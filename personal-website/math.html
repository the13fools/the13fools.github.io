Math teaches one how to see and appreciate beautiful thought. I have had the fortune to study the subject from world-class professors at Cornell, and spent a semester studying discrete math in Hungary, through the Budapest Semesters in Mathematics program.

My mathematical taste is tends to be synthetic, in that I like problems that let me get my hands on concrete examples. As such, I tend to be most drawn to problems in algebra and combinatorics. One problem which is particularly characteristic of my mathematical taste is Turan's Brickyard Conjecture. My work with Adam Marcus on this problem was awarded the Menger Prize from the American Mathematical Society.

I also have an interest in math education. I helped teach a class of advanced high school kids as a junior staff member at HCSSiM. Notes from the first three weeks of the program can be found here. During the second half of the summer I helped lead a class on Galois Theory.

I also spent a month teaching Math and English in a rural monastery school in Myanmar (Burma). This experience exposed me to the sorts of problems which arise in international development. Going forward I am interested in making some contribution in this space.
